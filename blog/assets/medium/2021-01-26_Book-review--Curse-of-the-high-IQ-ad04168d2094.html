Book review: Curse of the high IQ

This is a book that I have mixed feelings about. Curse of the high IQ (Clarey, 2016) attempts to explore the sociological aspects of intelligence, as measured by the infamous intelligence quotient latent variable that social scientists, both classical and contemporary, have pondered for decades. It is also something of a self-help book, offering up advice near the end to those who suffer the perceived drawbacks of having a “high IQ.” The book does have some positives, but nonetheless also has its drawbacks. A brief tour of the book

Remember, this is just a succinct summary. BUY THE DAMN BOOK!!!1 Link will be in the conclusion.

Inside the gentleman amateur’s laboratory

The book starts out with an introduction of the author’s associates. One associate is called Mike. He it at forty-three (43) years of age, operated a real estate business and was able to mitigate the calamitous effects of the housing crash,¹ and because of his skill in financial management, was “his own man [who] answered to no one.”² The author discusses another associate called Jeff, who was a major for the United States army and has a knack for financial impulse control. The author observed their emotional state to be “miserable,” and then came to the realization that their above-average IQ scores might be a causal agent regarding their emotional state. The author surveyed Mike, Tony and the rest of his associates’ IQ scores,³ and compiled a table. Armed with this dataset, the author is convinced that an above-average IQ is the defining causal agent in those mental ailments that we refer to as misery, depression and sadness.

The author then claims that having an above-average IQ can have drawbacks that complements its advantages, while promising to offer solutions to — at best — eliminate any pain and — at worst — merely reduce the pain to some degree.

To quantify the highly abstract phenomena that we call intelligence, the author advises the reader to take an IQ test,⁴ and pit themselves up against the rest of the population by standardizing the raw score.⁵ If the reader is indeed above-average, the explanations and solutions discussed in the author’s book will most likely apply to them.

The author gives examples of the behavior of individuals who score in the average range of IQ,⁶ such as the “idiot” in the convenience store buying lottery tickets with a check (Clarey, 2016, p. 25), the manager who is threatened by automation (Ibid) and the potential romantic partner who watches sports and reality television (Ibid, p. 26).

Because the hobbies, customs and indeed overall culture of those who have average IQ scores are radically different from those who have above-average IQ scores, and of the fact that happiness is derived from the company of other people (Ibid), those who score above-average in IQ scores are more likely to feel miserable because of the difficulties that they will face with interacting with the rest of the general population.

A dysgenic dystopia

The author then gives a short anecdote back in his high school years, where he and his classmates were attending a lecture given by a local politician. The politician then asked the audience “how many members of congress are there?”

The author then answered the lecturing politician, which prompted another classmate of his to respond with his own question concerning the carrying capacity of alcoholic beverages. The author was unable to work out the answer, which prompted a snarky reply from his antagonist.

In hindsight of this event, the author tries to guesstimate the young classmate’s IQ to be “around 95” (Clarey, 2016, p. 31). The author’s reasoning is that those who are concerned with what he considers to be plebeian hobbies or activities is most likely to have an average IQ score. The author also believes that an average IQ score would be considered mentally retarded when compared to someone with an above-average IQ score.

This parable of the young lad with an average IQ score is then used as a hook to discuss what the author believes to be an “idiocracy,” or a society where those of lesser intelligence call the shots. The author laments about how those with an average IQ score — which consists of about 68 per cent of the population⁷ — are concerned with mediocre entertainment and sports as opposed to the higher matters of economics, politics, the classical arts, the sciences and other ideals that define a Platonic republic.

The author also discusses the envy that is developed by those with an average IQ score. Those with an average IQ score will envy those with an above-average IQ. Envy them for their intelligence, for their looks, for their wealth and for whatever advantage that they have been blessed with.

Because of that tyranny of the majority, those of above-average IQ will be at significant social disadvantages. As children, they will be subjected to overt ridicule at school. As adults, they will be subjected to the passive-aggressiveness of their peers, and the overt aggressiveness of the capitalist who manages them. And when those with an above-average IQ manage to procure a good income and good wealth, they will be subjected to, what the author deems to be, a massive level of taxation.

The author also notes that those who have average IQ scores are more likely to reproduce than those who have above-average IQ scores. The author then foresees something of a dysgenic society, or an “idiocracy,” forming because those “retarded” average IQ individuals are reproducing quicker than the above-average IQ individuals. And because those with average IQ scores are more likely to vote “socialist” policies, the conclusion is that those with an above-average IQ will have to subsidize those with those with average IQ scores.

The author then makes a final lament (for now) about how mediocrity is defining the contemporary American zeitgeist.

The capitalist’s ideal society

The author discusses both the American education system, and the social aspects of the workplace. Starting with education, the author briefly discusses the history of American education, which was modeled after a pre-German, Prussian education system.

The American-Prussian education system was designed to create obedient workers that were needed to fill the ever-increasing demand for factory workers in the nineteenth century. The consequences of this education system included a susceptibility to form a blind following to authority, a reduction in autonomy in both the individual and societal level and perhaps even a retardation in the nation’s collective intelligence.⁸

The author would proceed to describe his experience in school. His experience having to deal with those cruel, yet mediocre, schoolteachers along with other classmates that he did not want to associate with. The author also elaborates on the political and economic aspects of schooling, explaining how schools try to create an artificial demand for auxiliary school-staff for the purposes of procuring more funding.

The author compares schooling to a thirteen-year prison sentence,⁹ and justifies this comparison with an observation that those final year, secondary-school students were filled with joy and glee to finally have graduated. He then discusses his hypothesis that political forces spearheaded by those average IQ scoring individuals are causing the banality.

The author then discusses schoolteachers, awarding their collective average IQ with the guesstimate of 102.¹⁰ The contemporary American schoolteacher has an sense of entitlement, is banal in their teaching methods, and is authoritarian in the sense that when discussing a topic — such as history, philosophy, English literature, et cetera — the subjective assessment of the schoolteacher is somehow closer to the truth than the student’s assessment merely because of the schoolteacher’s position.

The author also discusses those students in need of special education and those ne’er do wells. He hypothesizes that, the presence of special education and ne’er do wells can be used as a justification for the school system consuming more public funding.

The author discusses rebelling students, presumably with an above-average IQ, who are misdiagnosed. These rebelling students are apparently distinct from the ne’er do wells, as the author has written about them in a more positive tone and light. The rebelling students are misdiagnosed with needing some kind of psychiatric medication and are belittled by those of higher rank — like the schoolteacher or the school administrator. The author chalks up the apparent “stupidity” of the rebelling students to boredom, and attributes their low marks to boredom.

The author also discusses post-secondary education, referring to it as “high school version 2.0” (Clarey, 2016, p. 73), wanting to assure the reader that universities in contemporary America are merely an extension of secondary education, not as a distinct environment to acquire “critical thinking skills.”¹¹ The author also criticizes contemporary university professors, believing their intellectualism to be merely shallow and pedantic.

American-Prussian schools exist to train workers as opposed to a tool that will aide in the the development of critical thinking skills. The author has the reader understand that merely leaving the schooling environment does not imply that the reader’s presumed misery will come to an end, because of the notion that IQ remains stable for most people’s life.

Because of the economic burden caused by loans and other economic forces, those members of the working class, be they in a blue collar trades or a white collar profession, are at a disadvantage to the capitalists. When at work, capitalists will most likely treat their workers poorly and make unreasonable demands — particularly concerning their social skills. The author gives anecdotes about how the management of private companies value social skills as opposed to competency to do the assigned task that will turn over a profit, and in particular discusses a piece of financial automation that was rebuffed by his boss because of its potential of laying off other employees and posing a threat to the manager’s authority.

The author also discusses other coworkers, and their banality caused by their average IQ scores. Average IQ scoring coworkers can also introduce “office politics” into the job and how such office politics can be a deterrent to the goals and desires of above-average IQ individuals.

The author concludes the career aspect of life by discussing alternatives to traditional work. These alternatives include finding a job with a high demand for workers but limited supply (Clarey, 2016, p. 112–113), entrepreneurship (Ibid., p. 113–114) and minimalism (Ibid., p. 114–115).

The socially awkward minority

This is an interesting chapter, and I wont summarize it too much here, but I do want to discuss an interesting notion that the author brought up.

To briefly summarize this chapter, it discusses the socializing aspects that above-average IQ individuals will suffer when looking for casual associates, romantic partners, and when having children. The internet may alleviate some of these problems.

Clarey (2016, p. 136–155) discusses dating between cis men and cis women, and briefly discusses the variation hypothesis when applied to IQ. The chart below gives a visual intuition of the variation hypothesis:

First advanced by Darwin et al. (1871), the variability hypothesis states that given a continuous trait distributed on a normal curve, males would have a greater variance when compared to females. Or,

In this case, we are interested in the larger variance between males and females. Studies conducted by Hedges et al. (1995) and Machin et al. (2008) suggest that the variability hypothesis may indeed apply to humans — at least in terms of their IQ scores. In terms of their spacial intelligence,¹² Lynn (1996) estimated the average of men to be μ = 31.8 and its respective standard deviation to be σ = 11.31. As for women, they score an average of μ = 28.68 and a standard deviation of σ = 10.37.

If ρ < 1 then there are more girls in the IQ range of interest. Likewise, if ρ > 1 then there are more boys in the IQ range of interest.¹³ In the normal range of 100–115 IQ, for every 100 girls, there are only 79 boys. However, when we leave the normal range of IQ, we see a very interesting pattern:

  • In the range of 115–125 IQ, for every two (2) boys, we have one (1) girl.
  • In the range of 125–135 IQ, for every six (6) boys, we have one (1) girl.
  • And in the range of 135–140 IQ, for every thirty (30) boys, we have one (1) girl.

The ratio increases not linearly, but exponentially. The ρ will only get larger as the IQᵢ and IQf range increases.

If we assume that peoples interests, hobbies and lifestyle can be directly mapped to their IQ scores, then the consequence for the variability hypothesis applied to IQ distribution implies that many men and women will have a difficult time finding a romantic partner of the other gender.¹⁴

Unsatisfied with the literature, the author runs an investigation of depression within above-average IQ individuals and their causal agents.

He devises a bivariate linear regression model that assesses the relationship between the macroeconomic variables that are national average IQ and the respective nation’s suicide rates. After eliminating countries where those physiological necessities are not of a top priority (right chart), the author concludes a weak correlation between national average IQ and suicide rates:

This proposes that there may indeed be evidence of absence on the effects that IQ causes mental illness through the proxy macroeconometric response variable of suicide rates —though I will address this later.

The author then hypothesizes that Maslow’s hierarchy of needs best explains why those individuals, both of average IQ scores and above-average IQ scores, are struggling in life. They who can meet the physiological and safety requirements are more in a position to pursue the higher needs that include love, belonging, self-esteem, and self-actualization.

The author claims that those with above-average IQ scores are more likely to experiment with drugs. His explanation is that those who have worked out their problems are more likely to develop boredom and consume drugs to mitigate the boredom.

An intelligent person’s problems and their proposed solutions

The author delivers perhaps the final lament concerning the state of contemporary American society. He believes that this society, which is mostly comprised of individuals with an average IQ score, “does everything in its might to make sure smart people do not reach their full potential” (Clarey, 2016, p. 190). Such a society will drive those above-average IQ individuals to develop a misanthropic or nihilistic philosophy.

The author advises those readers who are of high intelligence, or are suspected of being of high intelligence, to do the following:

  • Assess their IQ,
  • Mentor their above-average IQ children,
  • Reevaluate themselves,
  • Achieve a formal education as soon as possible,
  • Prioritize other individuals in their lives,
  • Follow their intuition,
  • Position themselves to reduce the leverage that a capitalistic manager may have on them,
  • Pursue their own capitalistic ambitions,
  • And (possibly) consume recreational drugs.

The author ends the book by noting that some of the reader’s problems cannot be solved. The best advice that his book doles out can, at worst, merely mitigate the reader’s problems.

The good stuff

Some might find the book to come off as petty or self-serving. But, what really brings out the author’s authenticity is his ability to write from the heart. His book may not have the pedantic academic tone that some of us¹⁵ may be too familiar with, but it definitely is an ambitious and creative piece of work.

The book also attacks an important problem in society: mental illness. It has been shown that above-average IQ individuals are more likely to develop some kind of mild to profound learning disability or mental illness (Karpinski et al., 2018). While author believes that these learning disabilities and mental illnesses are over-diagnosed (Clarey, 2016, p. 66–67), this book can nonetheless serve as a piece of the much greater puzzle that is human cognition.

Furthermore, the author’s polemics against the educational establishment and large corporations are a pleasure to read. While I disagree with the author as to what causes such a system to exist,¹⁶ I strongly agree with him when he acknowledges that the system is broken. The stories about how schoolteachers were outsmarted by the quick thinking of the students, and how control freak managers are deeply flawed despite their apparent brilliance are just to die for.

Finally, some of the advice given was fairly decent. I like how the author encourages the reader to evaluate themselves and try to control those aspects of their lives that will enable them to mitigate any negative effects brought to them by society. My favorite piece of advice was the one where the author told the reader to collect government welfare and force large corporations to sustain their minimalist lifestyles 😂 — no really, I’m not being sarcastic. Large corporations should be funding the welfare state.¹⁷

What are IQ tests made of?

This is a book discussing IQ and its relationship to various social aspects of life. It would be nice, however, if we knew exactly what IQ tests are measuring. Unfortunately, the author did not discuss specifically how IQ batteries are constructed and what construct of intelligence that it attempts to measure. So, I will give a brief introduction so that we can get an idea of how IQ batteries are constructed and attempts to measure what theory of intelligence.

Twitter user @Race__Realist wrote an excellent article discussing the history and procedure of constructing IQ tests (here), though I will try to give a very brief description of how psychologists pick test battery items and determine the construct validity of their tests. Richardson (2000, p. 30) explains how psychologists, through application of their theoretical constructs of intelligence (and through trial and error) devise test items that correspond to the rankings of schoolteachers.

I wont focus too much on the psychological theory of IQ test construction, but rather the statistical methods to derive an estimate of Spearman’s g — the underlying factor that supposedly represents general cognitive ability. I will be going by the textbook Modern Psychometrics with R (Mair, 2018), and start by describing the dimensional reduction procedure of factor analysis to derive Spearman’s g. We have Pearson’s correlation coefficient:

The r is an index concerning the strength of a linear relationship between two random variables X and Y . As the r approaches +1, we say the relationship between X and Y has a strong positive correlation. As the r approaches -1, we say the relationship between X and Y has a strong negative correlation. And as the r approaches 0, we say there is no correlation between X and Y.

It is important to understand the context under which Spearman’s g was computed. Computers in the twentieth century were completely different from the computers that come to mind today. Today’s computers consist of fancy electronics, but those twentieth century computers consisted of soft tissue. A human, typically a woman, was in charge of working out the computational aspects of science and engineering research in the days of Spearman and the early quantitative psychologists.

Because of the limitations of computing power in Spearman’s time, other methods would need to be employed to compute his g-factor. The less costly computational method that Spearman used was the tetrad differences criterion (Steiger, n.d.), where he computed the Pearson correlation coefficients for each pair of items in the IQ battery.

Spearman would then segregate the correlation coefficients belonging to the same “category,” multiply all the correlation coefficients by one another for each category and find the difference between them:

If the tetrad differences worked out to zero, then, as Spearman argued, that would justify the factor’s validity. One of the implications of factor analysis is that a single item in the IQ battery could be used to estimate the overall underlying g-factor.

Again, this isn’t the most rigorous treatment of the history and construction of IQ tests, but keep two things in mind:

  • The test items in IQ tests are highly influenced by the rankings of schoolteachers…
  • … and that IQ tests measure this latent variable that is argued to have a strong variance if the tetrad differences work out to zero.

With that in mind, let’s move on to the author’s research methods.

Some critiques of the author’s research methods

As discussed previously, to find evidence that above-average IQ scores are a causal agent in those “negative” emotions, the author surveyed his friends. However, there are at least two problems with this approach.

First off, the author merely asked his friends what their IQ scores were (e.x. “Jeff! What’s your IQ,” Clarey, 2016, p. 10) as opposed to testing them. This is problematic because his friends, intentional or inadvertently, may be supplying him with inaccurate test scores. Furthermore, his friends may have been taking their IQ tests on a battery other than the Weschler. The self-reported scores from potentially different IQ batteries can be misleading.

Secondly, even if the IQ scores were all accurate and measured on the same scale, the author failed to devise a reliable metric of what counts as “misery” or “depression.” Because of this, the above-average IQ score’s could be mapped onto potentially different kinds of cognitive processes and behaviors, which may lead to fallacies of equivocation.

Third off, the author’s friends are not a representative sample of the true population. The sampling method could be suffering from some kind of selection bias, and the author’s sample of friends and their IQs are n = 15 Generally the rule of thumb of a minimum sample size to perform a hypothesis test with a z test statistics is somewhere between 20 and 30. This sampling method resembles the method of special pleading more than a rigorous estimation.

Another problem with the book’s research methods is the lack of references and citations. The author makes a lot assertions, like how “[p]eople with high IQ’s tend not to be able to sleep well and do their best work at night.” (Clarey, 2016, introduction) and how “there is a statistically significant correlation between IQ and fertility” (Ibid., ch. 2). There is no bibliography to reference these assertions, they are vaguely stated, and therefore it is difficult to accept these statements.

Finally, I would like to draw attention to a bit of statistical analysis done in chapter 6 of the book. The author attacked the problem of the relationship between IQ and mental illness. As previously discussed, in an effort to assess the relationship between IQ and mental illness, the author uses suicide rates by country as a proxy variable for mental illness.

The author reported an R² coefficient of ~0.1083, which he interpreted as a small variance. I do want to try out a polynomial function to see if we can improve on the R² estimate. Unfortunately, there are several problems with this research method. First off, the data used by Lynn and Vanhanen (2008) has been called into question. The Lynn Data Project (here) has challenged the poor quality of much of Lynn and Vanhanen’s (LV) research, so the author’s usage of LV’s research is most likely a case of “garbage in, garbage out” data analysis.

Second off, the author is working with populations in the aggregate. It would be a better idea if he could instead work with populations in an individual level. Conduct (or cite) a longitudinal study that tracks the relationship between IQ scores and probability of suicide over individual actor’s lives (see Page, 2018, ch. 4).

Third off, the author’s regression model is immune to falsification. While the author reported an R² of ~0.1083, the author did not define what would be considered a high R² or setup a hypothesis test with a clearly defined α cutoff point. Furthermore, how can we subject this regression model to testing (Page, 2018, ch. 6)?

And finally, one does not need to commit suicide to exhibit the qualities of mental illness, depression or general sadness. As Frankl (1984) and Karpinski et al. (2018) demonstrated, mentally ill people do not necessarily commit suicide.

Some criticism concerning the advice given

Apart from the author’s advice for those above-average IQ individuals to pursue their own capitalistic ambitions, I agree with all of the advice in his book. My only problem is that he recommends this advice only to those who score above-average in their IQ scores.

Again, we need to remember that IQ is just an “averaging out” of scores on a variety of IQ test items. It is possible to score extremely above average on the logical reasoning and three-dimensional spatial rotation items on the IQ test, but score below average on verbal and rote arithmetic items on the IQ test. For our hypothetical individual who scores vary on different IQ batteries, the overall IQ may end up in the normal range, masking their true potential (and weaknesses) in the process.

It is important for everyone to get, not just an IQ test, but any kind of test for mental illness or learning disability. Also, it is important to note that it is possible for individuals to score average on IQ tests and still show signs of talent (s.a. Julia Robinson).

Minor critiques concerning writing style

There are a few minor critiques concerning the writing style of Curse of the High IQ. One of them is the author’s habit of making references to contemporary (sub-)culture in America.

One example is when discussing the differences in needs between an infantry soldier and a retired medical specialist (Clarey, 2016, p. 164–165). The author references terrorist groups like the Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant (ISIS), and the United States Navy SEALs. Another example is when the author tells a story of a young bloke (Clarey, 2016, p. 29–32) who sneered at him for his knowledge of American civics. The author referenced the MTV program Beavis and Butthead when describing the manner in which the young bloke sneered.

The problem, in my opinion, with making too many (sub-)cultural references is that it takes away from the timelessness of the book. The book should be written to survive the decades and centuries that passed its publishing. The tendency to make these (sub-)cultural references make it more difficult for the book to survive after a few decades.

Another minor problem is with the grammar. I do agree with the author when he claims that grammatical mistakes in a composition are not a massive problem.¹⁸ However, if the grammar has serious problems to the point that the reader has difficulty understanding the message of the book, criticism is called for.

For example, in the author’s criticisms of a young lad who sneered at him, he claims that “this young boy was retarded” (Clarey, 2016, p. 30, emphasis mine). However, later in the book, the author implies that IQ scores are fixed (Ibid., p. 82). This can cause some confusion to the reader because when describing the sneering young bloke, the past tense use of “was” implies that it is possible for IQ scores to change. Because of the past tense, one may ask, “is the sneering young lad more intelligent now?”

Again, I tend to agree with the notion that spelling and grammar isn’t everything. Though bad spelling and grammar can become a problem if the intended message being conveyed is difficult to understand because of the bad spelling or grammar.
