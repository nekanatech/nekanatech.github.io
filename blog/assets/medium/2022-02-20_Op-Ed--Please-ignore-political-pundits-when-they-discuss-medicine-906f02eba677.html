Op-Ed: Please Ignore Political Pundits when they Discuss Medicine

Recently, an internet personality called Joe Rogan was responsible for spreading misinformation regarding the virus and the vaccine (An Open Letter to Spotify, 2022). This prompted a musician called Neil Young to issue an ultimatum to audio streamer Spotify: either remove Joe Rogan’s misinformation regarding the virus and vaccines, or else he, along with other like-minded followers and artists, will stop using their platform (Carman, 2022; Forde 2022; Knibbs, 2022). Spotify chose to keep Rogan and are allowing him to spread said misinformation, which prompted Young and others to stop using the audio streaming platform (Ibid; Young, 2022).

The internet has been described as “the repository of all human garbage” by Tina Fey (Migone, 2011) and this is especially true regarding internet advice on medicine. The Rogan-Young-Spotify debacle prompted me to write a brief but important article in hope that I can convince someone to either stop discussing medicine unless they have valid credentials — and even then. Also, I hope to convince the reader to not take medical advice from political pundits.

Power and Responsibility in Medicine

I want to make one thing clear: it is very difficult to hold figures on the internet, anonymous or not, legally accountable for their speech (unless said speech is libelous or directly threatening violence against an individual or group of individuals). Doctors, on the other hand, can be held accountable. Here are some examples:

  • P​erhaps the most infamous example in medicine and developmental psychology is former doctor Andrew Wakefield. He created a bogus study attempting to demonstrate vaccines as a causal agent in the development of autism. Wakefield lost his license because of this, and has resorted to promoting his fraudulent study on alternative media (Godlee et al., 2011).
  • Former medical doctor Scott McMahon was sentenced to 38 months in jail for “unlawful drug distribution [and identity theft].” McMahon also lost his medical license in the process (Department of Justice, 2021a).
  • Former medical doctor Verna Mae Lewis was sentenced to three years imprisonment for “over-prescribing Opioids” and was ordered to pay a total of $510,000 for what was called “illegal prescribing.” (Department of Justice, 2021b).
  • F​ormer medical doctor Javaid Perwaiz was sentenced to 59 years in prison for performing unnecessary medical procedures on patients and defrauding insurance companies for around $20 million dollars (Department of Justice, 2021c).

My method of listing examples of bad doctors losing their credentials due to bad or unethical practices may be invoking the method of special pleading, but I am not claiming that all bad doctors are punished for their actions. Rather, I am merely trying to demonstrate that there is a system in place to filter out bad doctors, and the medical profession gains its authority in no small part because of such a filtering system.

On the other hand, Joe Rogan and other political pundits cannot easily be held accountable, or “filtered out,” in the same manner that the medical doctor can by the judicial system. Rogan and political pundits are protected by “freedom of speech.” And because medical specialists can be held accountable for their advice and speech in professional relationships with their patients, they are the best authorities regarding the virus and its treatment. I urge the reader to value the advice of their doctor as opposed to the political pundit, as doing so will mitigate any harm done to both their individual self and the larger society.

L​ooking forward

W​hile the government may not be able to hold Joe Rogan and Spotify accountable through the judicial branch, we as civilians can hold them accountable indirectly by choosing to not consume their products or services (K. B. and C. T.,* personal communications). We can choose not to listen to Joe Rogan and we can choose to use alternative platforms to those who platform quacks. B​ut in order for this to work, we all need to do our part — or, rather, in this case, learn to break the bad habit of consuming propaganda and not partake in quackery.

This was a rather short article that was not very insightful, but sometimes the obvious needs to be stated. Unfortunately we live in a world where those who can be held accountable are scoffed at, whereas the charlatans have more influence. This piece is just my attempt at fighting back against anti-vaccine and anti-medical propaganda.
